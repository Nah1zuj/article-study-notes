# Material-Type Structures

Choose a narrative structure from the material type before applying discipline-specific adjustments. The final note should fit the source's intellectual shape, not force every source into the same template.

## Research Article

Use for empirical, theoretical, experimental, computational, historical, or argumentative journal articles.

Structure around:

- research problem
- prior gap or debate
- central claim or hypothesis
- method, corpus, model, archive, cases, or textual basis
- evidence and reasoning
- findings or argument results
- limitations
- contribution to the field

Avoid writing a section-by-section abstract. Reconstruct how the article moves from problem to contribution.

## Empirical Social Science Article

Use when the source depends on data, fieldwork, interviews, surveys, experiments, regressions, ethnography, or mixed methods.

Structure around:

- research question
- theoretical frame
- data and method
- variables, cases, or field site
- identification or interpretive strategy
- findings
- robustness, limitations, and scope
- theoretical or practical implications

Keep methods visible enough for the reader to know what the evidence can and cannot support.

## Technical AI Or Engineering Paper

Use when the source proposes a model, system, algorithm, benchmark, architecture, dataset, or engineering method.

Structure around:

- task or technical bottleneck
- prior approach and limitation
- core idea
- architecture, algorithm, or system flow
- training/data/evaluation setup
- results and ablations
- failure modes and limitations
- what the paper does and does not prove

Preserve formulas, pseudocode, architectural blocks, or evaluation tables when they are central to understanding.

## Review, Survey, Or Field Overview

Use `review-article-patterns.md`. The note should explain how the source maps a field, not summarize each cited work in sequence.

Structure around:

- why the field needs a review
- organizing principle
- major traditions, themes, methods, or debates
- what is known
- what remains unsettled
- taxonomy, framework, or research agenda
- limits of the review

## Theoretical Or Conceptual Essay

Use for theory-building, conceptual clarification, philosophical argument, legal theory, normative theory, or interpretive essays.

Structure around:

- conceptual problem
- key distinctions
- thesis
- argument sequence
- objections or rival positions
- replies or refinements
- implications
- remaining tensions

Do not convert the essay into a list of claims. The note should preserve conceptual movement.

## Book Chapter Or Textbook Chapter

Use when the source teaches a topic, introduces a field, or synthesizes background for learners.

Structure around:

- learning problem
- chapter map
- core concepts
- explanation sequence
- examples or applications
- important distinctions
- summary of what the chapter enables the reader to understand

Do not treat textbook exposition as original research unless the chapter makes an explicit scholarly intervention.

## Lecture Slides Or Course Handout

Use when the source is instructional and often fragmentary.

Structure around:

- lecture question or session topic
- route through the material
- concepts and definitions
- teacher's examples or emphasized distinctions
- underlying argument or explanatory order
- synthesis and review aids

If slides are bullet-heavy, infer the conceptual or argumentative order instead of preserving bullet order mechanically. If a syllabus, reading, or textbook supplies a better structure, use that as the controlling source.

## Classical, Primary, Legal, Or Canonical Text

Use when the source itself is a primary text, scripture, statute, classical passage, historical document, literary work, or canonical source.

Structure around:

- historical or interpretive problem
- key passage or textual unit
- terms and distinctions
- the author's own sequence, narrative movement, doctrine, rule, or image pattern
- interpretive dispute
- later reception or implications
- careful summary of what the text establishes

For classical or canonical texts, do not force argument reconstruction when the source is not organized as an argument. Follow the author's order and reconstruct the movement of the text itself. Use short quotations only where wording matters. Explain the quotation immediately; do not collect quotations in a separate dump.

## Literary Text

Use for fiction, poetry, drama, literary prose, memoir, or literary essays.

If the text is classic, canonical, or historically important, structure around:

- narrative structure or poetic sequence
- voice, perspective, and form
- imagery, motifs, symbols, and rhetoric
- character, scene, plot, or speaker movement
- historical and literary context
- interpretive tensions and reception
- what the work enables the reader to understand

If the text is not classic or canonical, structure around:

- central theme
- narrative or rhetorical setup
- major ideas and emotional movement
- style and imagery when important
- social, cultural, or personal stakes
- limitations or interpretive cautions

Do not reduce literary texts to moral lessons or plot summaries. Preserve how form creates meaning.

## Interview, Speech, Or Transcript

Use for interviews, podcasts, talks, lectures, speeches, oral histories, panel transcripts, or meeting transcripts.

Structure around:

- context and speaker position
- central question or occasion
- major claims, stories, or explanations
- sequence of topics
- examples and evidence
- tensions, omissions, or shifts in stance
- key takeaways

Separate what the speaker says from what can be inferred. Do not polish away the situated character of speech when it matters.

## News Feature, Longform Article, Or Magazine Essay

Use for journalistic longform, features, profiles, reported essays, public-facing explainers, or narrative nonfiction.

Structure around:

- central issue or story
- scene, case, or hook
- actors and institutions
- chronology or explanatory arc
- evidence and reported examples
- broader context
- what the article argues or implies
- limits, missing perspectives, or verification needs

Distinguish reporting, interpretation, and opinion. Do not treat journalistic framing as settled scholarship.

## Case Study

Use for business cases, policy cases, clinical cases, legal cases, historical cases, or organizational cases.

Structure around:

- situation and decision problem
- actors and constraints
- chronology or process
- evidence and turning points
- analytical framework
- what happened versus what the author claims it shows
- transferable lessons and limits

Separate case facts from interpretation and from generalizable lessons.

## Report, White Paper, Or Policy Document

Use when the source comes from an institution, public agency, company, think tank, lab, or professional body.

Structure around:

- institutional purpose
- problem definition
- evidence base
- framework or recommendations
- assumptions
- implementation conditions
- risks, omissions, and limits

Treat institutional claims as positioned claims. Do not mistake advocacy, compliance language, or product framing for settled scholarship.

## Multi-Source Folder

Use when the input is a folder or bundle rather than a single text.

Structure around:

- user's stated goal
- source hierarchy
- shared problem
- convergences and disagreements
- strongest evidence or textual basis
- integrated concept/argument map
- unresolved questions
- references

Do not produce one mini-summary per file unless the user asks for a dossier. Build an integrated note.
